Problem

A missed deadline is the moment when the problem becomes visible. But it can be addressed earlier — before an unstable structure triggers a chain reaction.

In an ETO environment, two projects may look identical in timelines, phases, and KPIs, yet differ greatly in their ability to absorb deviations. The difference does not emerge in reports, but in the execution structure: in dependencies, work density, sequencing, and flexibility already during development.

Why KPIs show the problem too late

Traditional KPIs capture outcomes after the execution structure has already taken shape. They show timelines, completion percentage, or budget, but they do not show whether the project itself can withstand a real deviation — a delivery delay, a design change, a lack of resources, or a shift between phases.

  • the project may look “on track,” yet already be structurally unstable
  • local overloads and rigid dependencies are not visible at the level of aggregated KPIs
  • the issue becomes visible only when fixing it is already expensive

Where the problem is actually created

The problem does not originate on the shop floor or in the final project report. It arises when the product structure and execution logic are formed: who depends on whom, where phases are stitched too tightly, where there is no buffer, and where the project loses its ability to adapt.

  • engineering makes decisions that already define downstream execution
  • the separation of roles and phases may look rational, yet still make the project unstable
  • if execution is not taken into account in R&D, the project becomes hostage to late corrections
Gleisen Logic

Project stability is defined not only by timelines, but by the combination of structure, dependencies, density, and flexibility

Gleisen views the project not as a set of separate tasks, but as a product structure over time. BOM, phases, dependencies, and work sequencing become one integrated system, used not only to assess stability in engineering, but also to support decisions in procurement, production, and installation.

Structure

BOM as the project foundation

The project is viewed through parts, assemblies, and their interrelationships — not only through abstract phases. This makes the actual logic of product formation visible.

This structure becomes shared across all departments and serves as a single project foundation.

Time

Timing in the context of structure

What matters is not only the dates themselves, but how time is distributed across dependent parts of the project, where dense sections emerge, and where there is no room for adaptation.

This makes it possible to make decisions at the assembly level rather than at the level of individual tasks or part numbers.

Stability

The ability to withstand deviations

Gleisen makes visible where the project will remain manageable under change, and where even a small deviation will lead to cascading disruptions.

This matters not only in R&D, but also during execution, when deviations have already occurred.

Gleisen R&D

Bringing execution understanding into the product development phase

Gleisen R&D makes it possible to see project stability when engineering decisions can still be changed — before procurement, before production, and before late costly corrections. At the same time, the same structure remains a practical tool after project launch — for downstream decisions across departments and during installation.

1. Project stability becomes visible before execution

The project structure becomes analyzable already in R&D: you can see where rigid dependencies, overloaded transitions between phases, and a lack of adaptability arise.

  • it becomes visible whether the project can withstand real deviations
  • it becomes visible where a small delay will trigger a cascading problem
  • it becomes visible which decisions make the project fragile even before the start

2. Engineering starts taking execution into account

Engineering decisions are no longer made separately from downstream reality. Development begins to take into account not only product design, but also the project’s ability to be executed.

  • execution becomes part of engineering, not a late reaction
  • the project structure can be adjusted while changes are still inexpensive
  • execution design is moved from late phases into R&D

3. The project structure is used during execution

After project launch, the same structure remains a working tool for departments. It makes it possible to understand the impact of deviations not at the level of individual parts, but at the level of assemblies and the entire project.

  • it becomes visible which parts of the project can continue despite delays
  • it becomes visible which assemblies will lead to downstream process blockages
  • decisions are made at the structure level, not at the level of individual part numbers
